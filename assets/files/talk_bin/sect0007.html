4.3 Quasitriangular structures

Next we give a few important definitions that crop up regularly in the theory of Hopf algebras.

Definition 4.9

  • A Hopf algebra is commutative if its underlying algebra is commutative, i.e. m∘τ=mm\circ \tau =mm∘τ=m.

  • A Hopf algebra is cocommutative if its underlying coalgebra satisfies τ∘△=△\tau \circ \triangle =\triangle τ∘△=△.

  • A non-zero x∈Hx\in Hx∈H is group-like if △(x)=x⊗x\triangle (x)=x\otimes x△(x)=x⊗x. (Compare this with the coproduct on a group algebra!)

  • An element x∈Hx\in Hx∈H is primitive if △(x)=x⊗1+1⊗x\triangle (x)=x\otimes 1+1\otimes x△(x)=x⊗1+1⊗x.

The set of grouplikes inside a Hopf algbra form a group, whilst the set of primitives are somewhat like elements of a Lie algebra. , whilst the set of primitives forms a Lie algebra with respect to commutator product [x,y]:=x⋅y−y⋅x[x,y]:=x\cdot y-y\cdot x[x,y]:=x⋅y−y⋅x. As we stated above, the universal enveloping algebra U(g)U(\mathfrak {g})U(g) of Lie algebra g\mathfrak {g}g is a Hopf algebra, and the subset of primitives is precisely g\mathfrak {g}g. An important theorem of Cartier-Konstant-Milnor-Moore states that every cocommutative Hopf algebras has a decomposition into a (smash) product of the universal enveloping algebra of the Lie algebra formed by its primitive elements with the group algebra created by its group-like elements. Hence cocommutative Hopf algebras can be viewed as structures that merge group theory with Lie theory.
